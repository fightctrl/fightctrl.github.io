THE PROJECT

Have You Eaten Yet? is a personal cookbook of recipes from my family. It contains recipes of traditional dishes my parents grew up eating in Malaysia and simple home-cooked meals we’ve eaten together as a family.

My way of recording my family's recipes to prevent further loss...

RE-ESTABLISHING CULTURAL TIES

Family recipes are usually passed down through practice and oral tradition but language and geographical barriers make it difficult to preserve familial traditions for diasporic groups. My parents have already lost some recipes from their parents as it was never written down or recorded.

As someone whose connection to culture and heritage is hanging on by a thread, food has been the only thing that’s stuck with me. This cookbook is my way of recording my family’s recipes to prevent further loss. This cookbook also aims to provide insight into Malaysian food for diaspora and non-Malaysians by including original photography and short personal anecdotes.

DISCOVERING COOKBOOK TRENDS

I looked into recent cookbooks and their reviews to find out what people find appealing about them from a design and information perspective.

I discovered that cookbooks that take a more personal approach tend to stand out more. But I found that audiences are split on their opinion of personal anecdotes as some of them want cookbooks to be purely practical.

From all this analysis, I decided that recipes should be the main focus and to make the design approachable because cookbooks are mainly used by audiences for its function. But small personal snippets, interesting layouts and appealing photography will also be included as they make the cookbook distinct from others and feel lived-in.

NOT JUST DESIGNING FOR MYSELF

Even though this is primarily a personal project, design should be used to be inclusive and accommodating to others. So I created personas and empathy maps of diasporic and non-diasporic audiences to help me in the direction could cookbook should take in terms of construction and design.

Design should be used to be inclusive and accommodating to others...

EMBEDDING CULTURAL IDENTITY

I wanted a way of creating a subtle visual link to my family’s culture because I knew I also wanted to include recipes of non-traditional dishes as well (even though the way my family makes it would still be informed by our culture).

The best way of communicating the cookbook’s cultural association was using design commonly found in my family’s hometown, George Town in Penang. And one of the most distinct pieces of design in that area are its tile patterns.

REVISING, REVISING, REVISING

Trying to creating dynamic looking layouts for each recipe whilst still ensuring the contents flowed was challenging.

From my initial plain and repetitive layouts, I managed to create purposeful and appealing layouts by letting images bleed off the page and filling unnecessary white space with the pattern designs.

EVALUATING THE PROJECT

Creating this cookbook was quite the family activity with how involved everyone was. I didn’t realise just how important food was to us until I started working on this project.

There is room for improvements like the photography, especially food photography as that was surprisingly hard and really affected the layouts I could create.

Ideally, I would have also liked to have gotten this book printed and bound as hardcover as it’s able to lay flat and protect the book better, especially when used in a kitchen. But that wouldn’t have been a financially smart choice when I was only getting a few copies printed.

Even though I barely cook, this cookbook is something I’ll keep using when I’m in need of something familiar. There is also the possibility of adding more recipes as this cookbook is not a comprehensive collection.
